\title{A Lightweight, Reproducible Transfer-Learning Pipeline for Sentinel-2
Land-Cover Classification and Cross-Frontier Deforestation Detection}

\author{Joshua Anojulu% <-- update affiliation/email before submission
\thanks{Code and data: \url{https://github.com/Joshua-Anojulu/satellite-deforestation-classifier}}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Deforestation monitoring requires automated tools that are accurate,
reproducible, and cheap to run. We fine-tune a ResNet50 convolutional neural
network, pretrained on ImageNet, on the EuroSAT Sentinel-2 land-cover dataset,
then apply the classifier to multi-date Sentinel-2 imagery to detect forest
loss. The classifier reaches 98.26\% accuracy on a held-out EuroSAT test set
(macro-F1 0.982), with near-perfect forest recall (0.997). Applied to two
independent Amazonian deforestation frontiers---Rond\^onia and S\~ao F\'elix do
Xingu, Brazil---across 2016 and 2024, it detects Forest$\rightarrow$non-Forest
transitions benchmarked against Global Forest Watch (Hansen Global Forest
Change). The detector is high-precision and coarse: 82--99\% of detected losses
are confirmed by Global Forest Watch, and recall for wholesale clearings reaches
71\%, while recall against all reported loss is low because the 640\,m patch
resolution cannot resolve sub-patch losses that 30\,m analysis captures. The
method generalizes across both frontiers, performing better where clearings are
larger and cleaner. We further report a critical, often-overlooked finding: a
naive application of the EuroSAT-trained model to modern atmospherically
corrected imagery fails badly (72\% of rainforest misclassified as water) until
per-channel radiometric \emph{moment matching} aligns the imagery to EuroSAT's
statistics.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Remote sensing, land-cover classification, transfer learning, deforestation,

\section{Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{T}{ropical} deforestation is a leading driver of biodiversity loss
and carbon emissions, with sustained global tree-cover loss over the past two
decades~\cite{hansen2013}, much of it driven by commodity agriculture and pasture
expansion~\cite{curtis2018,fao2020}. Authoritative monitoring---most prominently
Global Forest Watch, built on the Hansen Global Forest Change (GFC)
product---relies on large-scale pipelines. A complementary question for students
and small labs is whether a \emph{lightweight, reproducible} convolutional model,
trainable in under half an hour on a single consumer GPU, can recover a useful
deforestation signal, and how it compares to the authoritative product.

This work contributes: (1) a reproducible EuroSAT classifier (ResNet50 transfer
learning) reaching 98.26\% test accuracy with common preprocessing pitfalls
corrected; (2) an end-to-end, georeferenced change-detection pipeline evaluated
on \emph{two} independent study areas; and (3) a clear demonstration---and
fix---of the radiometric domain shift that silently breaks EuroSAT-trained models
on modern Sentinel-2 Level-2A imagery.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Dataset}
EuroSAT~\cite{helber2019} contains 27{,}000 Sentinel-2 patches (64$\times$64\,px
at 10\,m/px) in 10 land-cover classes; we use the RGB version. We split 80/10/10
(seed 42). Transforms are applied \emph{per split}: training receives random
horizontal/vertical flips (valid for nadir imagery); validation/test receive
none. A common tutorial error attaches one transform before splitting, leaking
augmentation into evaluation or denying it to training; we avoid this with a
per-split wrapper. Images are resized to 224$\times$224 and normalized with
ImageNet statistics.

\subsection{Model and Training}
We load ResNet50~\cite{he2016} with ImageNet~\cite{deng2009} weights
(\texttt{weights=ResNet50\_Weights.DEFAULT}) and replace the final layer with a
10-way head. Training is two-phase: (i) backbone frozen, head trained 10 epochs
(Adam, lr $10^{-3}$); (ii) all layers fine-tuned 8 epochs (lr $10^{-4}$). We use
cross-entropy loss, batch size 64, automatic mixed precision, and retain the best
checkpoint by validation accuracy. Training took $\sim$20\,min on an NVIDIA RTX
4060 (8\,GB), CUDA 12.6, PyTorch 2.12, fixed seed.

\subsection{Study Areas and Imagery}
We selected two documented Amazonian frontiers, confirmed on the Global Forest
Watch loss layer: Rond\^onia (Ariquemes ``fishbone'' frontier) and S\~ao F\'elix
do Xingu, Par\'a (a cattle-driven, high-deforestation municipality). For each we
retrieved cloud-masked ($\leq$20\%) median true-color (B04, B03, B02) Sentinel-2
Level-2A composites~\cite{drusch2012} for the 2016 and 2024 dry seasons
(June--September) from the Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem via openEO. The same
seasonal window each year avoids phenology-driven false change. Scenes are
$\sim$2743$\times$2433\,px (Rond\^onia, UTM 20S) and $\sim$2770$\times$2439\,px
(S\~ao F\'elix, UTM 22S).

\subsection{Radiometric Moment Matching}
EuroSAT derives from hazier, less atmospherically corrected imagery with a blue
cast (global RGB mean $\approx(86,97,103)$). Modern L2A composites are
atmospherically corrected, so a naive render is catastrophically misclassified
(72\% of a rainforest scene labeled water). We correct this by per-channel moment
matching: each scene channel is linearly rescaled so its mean and standard
deviation match EuroSAT's global statistics. Each date is matched to the same
reference, also normalizing the two dates to a common radiometry.

\subsection{Change Detection and Validation}
Each scene is tiled into georeferenced 64$\times$64 (640\,m) patches, sliding the
window with a 32\,px stride ($\sim$2$\times$ finer grid) while preserving the
model's 640\,m footprint. A cell is flagged as deforestation if classified Forest
in 2016 and \{AnnualCrop, Pasture, Industrial, Residential, PermanentCrop\} in
2024. An optional softmax-confidence gate ($\geq$0.7 both dates) yields a
higher-precision variant. We validate against the Hansen GFC-2024-v1.12
\texttt{lossyear} raster, aggregating it to our grid: a reference cell is ``loss''
if a fraction $f$ of its 30\,m pixels record loss in $(2016,2024]$; we sweep
$f\in\{0,0.25,0.5,0.75\}$.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Classification}
The classifier reaches 98.26\% test accuracy (2700 images; best validation
98.52\%; macro-F1 0.982), all per-class F1 $\geq$0.97. Frequent confusions are
interpretable: River$\rightarrow$Highway (thin linear features), among
HerbaceousVegetation/AnnualCrop/Pasture (green ground cover), and
Industrial$\leftrightarrow$Residential. Forest recall is 0.997, essential for the
downstream task (Fig.~\ref{fig:cm}).

\subsection{Deforestation Detection and Validation}
On the finer grid the detector flagged 152 loss cells in Rond\^onia and 195 in
S\~ao F\'elix, concentrated at forest edges (Figs.~\ref{fig:rond}--\ref{fig:saof}).
Validation (Table~\ref{tab:gfw}) shows high precision and resolution-limited
recall: at any-loss, 82\% (Rond\^onia) and 99\% (S\~ao F\'elix) of detections are
confirmed; for wholesale clearings ($\geq$75\% of a cell) recall reaches 71\%
(Rond\^onia). Low recall against all loss is inherent to the 640\,m resolution.

\subsection{Operating Point and Generalization}
A confidence gate trades recall for precision (e.g., Rond\^onia
0.45$\rightarrow$0.55 at $\geq$25\%), giving a tunable high-precision alert mode.
Across both frontiers the pipeline validates against GFW, performing better on
S\~ao F\'elix's larger, cleaner clearings---evidence of generalization, with
agreement tracking clearing morphology.

\section{Discussion}
The more important lesson than benchmark accuracy is that it did not transfer for
free: without radiometric matching, a 98\%-accurate model produced meaningless
output. As a detector the system is a high-precision, coarse-resolution screening
tool---reliable when it flags a location, but under-reporting small or selective
losses. Limitations: (i) European training applied to tropical Brazil (domain
shift); (ii) 640\,m patches miss small/linear clearings, capping recall; (iii)
RGB-only input discards informative spectral bands; (iv) residual cloud and
phenology can cause false change; (v) moment matching approximates, but does not
reproduce, EuroSAT radiometry.

\section{Conclusion}
A lightweight, reproducible ResNet50/EuroSAT pipeline recovers a deforestation
signal that agrees strongly in precision with Global Forest Watch across two
independent Amazonian frontiers, once radiometric domain shift is corrected.
Future work: all 13 Sentinel-2 bands; region-matched fine-tuning; finer patches
or segmentation for recall; and multi-temporal sequences beyond two snapshots.
